# Icebreaker Framework

## The Rule

The icebreaker has one job: make them think "this person actually looked at my business." It should feel like something a friend or peer would say, not something a salesperson would write.

## The Formula

**[Saw/Noticed/Read] + [specific thing they did] + [why it caught your eye]**

Two parts. What you saw. Why it matters. One sentence, occasionally two.

**When using two paragraphs:** Para 2 must extend the same observation as para 1. It is not a second observation about a different part of their site. It supports para 1 and bridges into the pitch.

WRONG: Para 1 about their TV production work. Para 2 about their client covenant page. Two different topics. Feels like a list.
RIGHT: Para 1 about their TV production work. Para 2: "Work that good deserves a pipeline to match." Same thread. Natural bridge.

---

## What Makes a Good Icebreaker

**Specific.** References something only they did. Not something that applies to 100 businesses.

**Verifiable.** They can confirm you actually saw it — a LinkedIn post, a case study, a project, a hire, a podcast appearance.

**Genuine.** You actually found it interesting. If you have to fake the compliment, pick a different prospect.

**Casual.** Written like a text to a colleague, not a cover letter.

**About what they DID, not what they ARE.** "Saw you placed 40 people at Deloitte last quarter" not "you're clearly a talented recruiter."

## Where to Find Material (in order of quality)

1. **Intent signals and social listening (strongest)** — comments on Reddit, YouTube, industry forums, competitor pages, Slack communities, Skool groups. Reveal what they're actively thinking about right now.
2. **Their recent LinkedIn posts** — what they wrote, shared, or commented on. Posts reveal priorities, frustrations, and wins.
3. **Their website (go deep, not surface)** — a specific case study, a particular portfolio project, a testimonial from a named client, their about page story. NOT the homepage headline.
4. **Press or podcast appearances** — interviews, features, articles about or by them.
5. **A recent hire or team change** — visible on LinkedIn company page.
6. **Their company news** — funding, new service, new market, expansion.
7. **Something they built or launched** — a tool, a resource, an event, a side project.

**Avoid:** LinkedIn headline, job title, company tagline, follower count, years in business. These prove nothing.

## The Personal Proof Mechanic (makes it undeniable)

The strongest icebreakers include a small personal detail that proves the reaction is real — not a compliment about the work, but a detail about YOUR experience with the work that no salesperson would bother to include.

**The mechanic:** [What you noticed] + [small personal detail that proves you actually engaged]

The detail should be slightly voluntary, slightly unnecessary, and impossible to template.

### Examples:

**Branding agency:**
"Went through your portfolio last night when I should have been sleeping. The identity you did for [client] stopped me mid-scroll. The way the typography carries the whole brand without relying on the logo is really well considered."

**UX agency:**
"I actually signed up for [client]'s product just to try the onboarding flow you redesigned. Took me 90 seconds to get through it. That's annoyingly good."

**PR agency:**
"Read the [publication] piece you landed for [client] over coffee this morning. Sent it to a friend who works in comms and her exact response was 'how did they get that placement.' Take that as a compliment."

**Consulting firm:**
"Your case study on [client] has been sitting in my bookmarks for a week. The bit about restructuring their ops team from 14 to 8 while increasing output is the kind of thing most consultancies would never publish because it makes it look too easy."

**From a LinkedIn post:**
"Your post about [topic] has been stuck in my head since Tuesday. Specifically the line about [specific phrase]. I've caught myself repeating it in two separate conversations this week."

### Why these work — each has a personal detail that can't be templated:
- "When I should have been sleeping" — proves you were actually browsing their work at night
- "I actually signed up just to try it" — proves you went beyond their website
- "Sent it to a friend" — proves you thought it was good enough to share
- "Sitting in my bookmarks for a week" — proves ongoing interest, not a 3-second glance
- "Caught myself repeating it in conversations" — proves the content actually impacted you

**The test:** Could this personal detail apply to anyone's work? If yes, it's not personal enough.

## Patterns That Work

### Pattern 1: Intent signal (strongest)
"Saw your comment on [creator]'s video about scaling outbound. Sounds like you've been thinking about this."
"Read your question in [community] about finding clients beyond referrals. Hit close to home for me."

### Pattern 2: Website deep-dive
"Went through your case studies. The [specific project] for [specific client] stood out — especially the [specific result or detail]."
"Looked at your portfolio. The [specific project] is genuinely better than what most agencies twice your size are putting out."

### Pattern 3: Saw the work + genuine reaction
"Saw the identity you did for [client]. The colour palette is really well considered."
"Watched the product film you shot for [client]. Felt more like a short film than a brand video."

### Pattern 4: Noticed a move + what it signals
"Noticed you just landed [client/contract]. Not easy to win in that space."
"Saw your team grew from 4 to 11 this year. Scaling that fast without losing quality says a lot."

### Pattern 5: Consumed their content + specific takeaway
"Read your post on [topic]. The point about [specific detail] stuck with me."
"Listened to your episode on [podcast]. Your take on [specific point] was refreshingly honest."

### Pattern 6: Observed something about their business + insight
"Looked at your client list. Heavy on [industry]. Clearly carved out a strong niche there."

### Pattern 7: Referenced a shared connection or context
"Saw you spoke at [event]. Wish I'd caught it."
"Noticed we're both in [community]. Your post about [topic] stood out."

## Tone Scale

**Too corporate:** "I was very impressed by the exceptional work your team delivered on the recent XYZ project."
**Too sycophantic:** "Oh my god, your rebrand for that wellness company was INCREDIBLE. Seriously blown away."
**Too vague:** "Love what you're doing at Bright Strategy. Really great stuff."

**Just right:** "Saw the rebrand you did for that wellness company. The packaging alone would make me pick it off a shelf."

The sweet spot is **specific + understated**. You noticed something. You're mentioning it. You're not gushing.

---

## The AI-at-Scale Safeguard

Read 5 icebreakers in a row. If they all start the same way ("Saw your..." / "Noticed your..."), the pattern is visible. Vary the structure:

- Start with what you saw: "Went through your portfolio last night."
- Start with the reaction: "The onboarding flow you designed is annoyingly good."
- Start with a personal detail: "Sent your [publication] piece to a friend this morning."
- Start with a question: "How did you land [client]? That's not an easy account to win."
- Start with a specific detail: "The typography on the [client] identity carries the whole brand."

---

## Tone Rules (non-negotiable)

**No analysis. Just reactions.**
- Bad: "Your strategic approach to brand positioning demonstrates clear expertise."
- Good: "The packaging you did for [client] would genuinely make me pick it off a shelf."

Analysis tells them what you concluded. Reactions tell them how you responded. Reactions are human. Analysis is corporate.

**First draft is always too formal. Edit down.**
"I was really impressed by" → "loved"
"I noticed that your company recently" → "saw you just"

**Contractions always.**
"I have been following" → "I've been following"

**Banned words:** impressed, remarkable, exceptional, "I came across", "I stumbled upon", "I wanted to reach out", "I hope this finds you well", "I'd love to connect", "incredible work", "amazing" (without specifics), "your team" (when you mean them personally), "I believe/I think" before a compliment, analysis/reviewed/evaluated, leverage/utilise/optimise, synergy/alignment/strategic, "just wanted to", "quick question" as opener.

**Words that sound human:** saw / noticed / read / watched / tried / loved / really liked / stood out / sharp / clean / solid / smart move / stuck with me / got me thinking / caught my eye / honestly / genuinely (sparingly) / annoyingly good / hard to argue with / night and day.

## What to Avoid

- **Database reveal:** "Found [firm] while looking at firms that [category]" — exposes the prospecting process. Just state what you saw.
- **Process announcement:** "Read through the site before writing", "Dug into X before reaching out" — the specificity of what you say proves you looked. Don't announce it.
- **The fake compliment:** "I'm really impressed by your company's mission and values."
- **The LinkedIn headline reference:** "I see you're the founder of a branding agency." They know that.
- **The generic opener:** "Love what you guys are doing." What specifically?
- **The compliment + pivot:** "Amazing work! Speaking of which..." — the pivot kills sincerity.
- **The diagnosis disguised as research:** "I noticed your agency doesn't seem to have an outbound system..." — puts them on the defensive.
- **Follower count, company size, revenue:** "Congrats on hitting 10K followers!" — bot energy.
- **Rankings as hooks:** Inc. 5000, Forbes lists, Top 10 features — not buying signals, feel like flattery.

## The Bridge: Transitioning From Icebreaker to Pitch

The bridge connects what you observed to why you're reaching out. Without it, the icebreaker feels random and the pitch feels like a bait-and-switch.

### Bridges that work:

From complimenting their work:
- "That kind of result is exactly what makes outbound work well — real proof that writes the outreach for you."
- "With results like that, your pipeline shouldn't depend on referrals."
- "That level of specificity is rare, which is actually what made me reach out."

From shared experience:
- "That's the problem I'm solving now."
- "That's exactly why I built this."

From their business move:
- "If pipeline is part of that plan, this might be well timed."
- "The hard part is usually getting it in front of the right people fast enough."

### Bridges that DON'T work:
- "Anyway, the reason I'm reaching out is..." — kills momentum. Signals the compliment was setup.
- "Speaking of which..." — transparent pivot.
- "On a completely different note..." — admits the icebreaker has nothing to do with the pitch.

The best bridges don't announce themselves. The reader flows from observation to pitch without noticing the transition. If you can feel the pivot, rewrite the bridge.

## Full Examples (icebreaker + bridge + pitch)

### From portfolio/case study:

**Production/video agency (Tom Wanek / WAYNIK):**
Been watching some of the WAYNIK TV spots this morning. Really enjoyed those. Impressive production for home services businesses. Your clients come across really well.

Work that good deserves a pipeline to match.

I build AI outreach systems for marketing agencies. It finds your ideal clients, writes outreach in your voice, and books client meetings into your calendar. No added headcount. Runs on autopilot.

Want me to send you a quick Loom showing how I'd build this for WAYNIK?

**Branding agency:**
Went through your portfolio last night. The identity you did for [client] stopped me mid-scroll. The way the typography carries the whole brand without relying on the logo is really well considered.

That kind of work clearly attracts clients who care about quality over cost.

This is a bit out there, but I build AI outreach systems for {{niche}}...

**UX agency:**
I actually signed up for [client]'s product just to try the onboarding flow you redesigned. Took me 90 seconds to get through it. That's annoyingly good.

With results like that, your pipeline shouldn't depend on referrals.

This is a bit out there, but I build AI outreach systems for {{niche}}...

### From LinkedIn posts:

**Founder who posted about feast-or-famine pipeline:**
Saw your post last week about the pipeline rollercoaster. The line about "best month ever followed by two months of nothing" — I could have written that myself when I was running my agency.

That's exactly why I built this.

This is a bit out there, but I build AI outreach systems for {{niche}}...

### From intent signals:

**Key difference from portfolio examples:** Para 1 is one sentence. The signal does all the work. Para 2 answers "why now" not "why you." No compliment needed.

**Hiring a revenue role:**
Saw [Agency] posted a Head of Business Development role last week. Sounds like new business has become a priority.

Might be worth a conversation before that hire starts.

**Recent acquisition:**
Saw [Agency] acquired [Studio Name] last month. Bigger team, bigger capability set.

Pipeline usually needs to keep up with a move like that.

**Founder asking about outbound in a community:**
Saw your question in [community] about getting clients beyond referrals. Most of the answers in that thread were generic. "Just post more on LinkedIn" doesn't pay the bills next month.

I've been working on something that actually solves this.

This is a bit out there, but I build AI outreach systems for {{niche}}...
