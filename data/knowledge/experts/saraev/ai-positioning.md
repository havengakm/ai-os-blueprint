# Nick Saraev — AI Positioning & Offer Strategy

Source: Nick Saraev (LeftClick, Maker School). Several million in AI product/service sales.

---

## Principle 1: AI makes things profitable, not possible

AI doesn't make things fundamentally go from impossible to possible. It takes pre-existing processes that are profitable and makes them faster and cheaper.

Don't try creating new AI products and services. Take a pre-existing process and add AI to it.

Example: Personalised email outreach was always possible (spend 25 minutes researching someone's Facebook feed). AI makes it profitable at 10,000 contacts instead of 5.

**How to apply:** Position CLYMB's system as "the outbound process you'd build anyway, just AI-powered." Not a new category. An existing category done profitably for the first time.

---

## Principle 2: AI is best at high-volume, low average order value

A 5% drop in conversion rate on a $25K deal = $125K/month lost. Not worth automating.

But a 5% drop on a $500 deal across 1,000 leads? Math works out because you're replacing 10-15 people.

Don't throw AI at high-stakes, low-volume sales processes. Use it where volume matters.

**How to apply:** Cold email outreach is the perfect use case. High volume (500+ contacts/month), low per-contact cost, small conversion differences offset by massive efficiency gains. Don't position the system as replacing closers. Position it as replacing the top-of-funnel grind.

---

## Principle 3: The only real moat is distribution

AI commoditises products. Anyone can build a Netflix clone. The only value is the customer base.

The most important skill is demand generation (marketing, top of funnel, cold email, ads, content).

Cold email generates distribution from nothing with the right messaging.

**How to apply:** Frame CLYMB as "building your distribution engine" not "automating your outreach." The system builds the one thing that actually matters: a pipeline of people who know you exist.

## Copy Patterns from Nick's Templates

### Pattern 1: The Referral Angle
Short, aligns incentives, performance-based. "If I could help you connect to people looking for [service] in exchange for a commission on closed rev, would you be open?"

### Pattern 2: The System Demo
Niche-specific, describes the system in plain English, exclusivity ("you and a Vancouver firm are the first").

### Pattern 3: The Lead Generation Offer
Outcome-based. "Would you be open to a few leads?" Hard to say no to.

### Pattern 4: The Fan + Offer
Super personalised (despite zero actual personalisation). Leads with social proof then a killer offer.

### Pattern 5: The Grateful Fan + Revenue Audit
Relationship-based. "You helped me, let me help you." Specific dollar amounts make the guarantee real.

### Key patterns across all templates:
1. Performance/risk-reversal: "I work mostly on performance"
2. Specific social proof: "$10M total", "$2M/yr firm" (not vague)
3. Casual tone: "yo yo", "ngl", lowercase, contractions
4. Exclusivity: "you and a Vancouver firm are the first"
5. Describe the system in plain English
6. Always a next step: "give you a quick ring"
