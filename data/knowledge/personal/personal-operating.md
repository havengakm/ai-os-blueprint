# Personal Operating Context: Kirsten Havenga

PERSONAL OPERATING CONTEXT
Kirsten Havenga
A living reference for how I think, work, decide, and operate. Use this to design systems, choose opportunities, build partnerships, and stay grounded.
1. COGNITIVE PROFILE
How Your Brain Works
You suspect ADHD (undiagnosed, currently on Ritalin 30mg and Serdep). This shapes everything about how you operate. It is not a flaw to fix. It is a cognitive style to design around.
Task Initiation
Your primary barrier is starting, not doing. Once you begin a task, you can sustain focus and produce high-quality work. The friction is in the transition from not-doing to doing. This manifests as procrastination, but it is an executive function challenge, not a motivation problem.
You default to comfortable tasks over important ones. Given a choice between building a system (dopamine-rich, feels productive) and sending 10 outbound DMs (uncomfortable, uncertain, genuinely productive), you will choose the system build every time. You are aware of this pattern. The solution is environmental: make the important task the path of least resistance.
Systems Thinking
You think in systems. Your natural instinct when facing any problem is to build a framework, categorise it, create a spreadsheet, design a process. This is both a strength (you can design sophisticated solutions) and a vulnerability (you spend more time designing the system than using it). Your wishlist spreadsheet has 13 tabs with scoring matrices. You need simplicity enforced externally: one piece of paper, three tasks, done.
Processing Style
Your brain needs input to settle. Silence amplifies mental chatter. You cannot meditate in the traditional sense. You cannot journal. Your brain processes by consuming and by talking, not by reflecting in stillness. Podcasts, audiobooks, conversations, movement: these are your processing tools. Any system that requires quiet introspection as a daily habit will be abandoned within days.
You process externally. Talking through problems (with people, with AI, out loud) produces clarity that sitting and thinking does not. You need sounding boards, not solitude. You make more decisions in conversation than in weeks of internal deliberation.
Thinking in Motion
You think best when moving. Walking, driving, cooking, standing at a counter. Your best ideas and clearest thinking do not happen at your desk. They happen when your body is engaged in something low-demand and your brain is free to wander. Scheduled movement is not just for fitness. It is a cognitive tool.
Analysis and Decision-Making
You have a strong analytical mind. You research thoroughly, compare options exhaustively, and weigh decisions carefully. The downside: analysis paralysis. You can spend weeks researching the perfect sports bra (with comparison tables across four stores) but struggle to send a single cold email. The depth of your analysis is inversely proportional to the discomfort of the task you are avoiding.
You are a conviction-led decision maker. Your confidence rises when proof exists and done is defined. Your confidence collapses when authority is unclear, success criteria are vague, or outcomes are ambiguous. You do not need motivation. You need clarity.
2. ENERGY AND RHYTHM
Daily Energy Map
Pre-peak (before 10am): Functional but not firing. Good for follow-ups, email, scheduling, light admin. Medium-energy work. Do not waste this on deep creative tasks. Do not waste it on scrolling.
Peak (10am-12pm): Your sharpest window. This is when you are most creative, most capable of difficult work, and most able to overcome initiation friction. Revenue-generating tasks, outbound, content creation, system design. Protect this window above all else.
Post-lunch (1pm-3pm): Your worst window. Energy drops, focus fades, resistance to hard tasks peaks. This is where you default to comfortable tasks or lose the day. The midday walk exists to break this pattern. After walking, you get a second wind, but it is medium energy at best.
Afternoon (3pm-5pm): Low to medium energy. Admin, research, organising, light build work. Wind down the work day. Plan tomorrow.
Evening: Your brain does not have an off switch. It runs hot all day and continues into the night. Low-stimulation audio bridges the gap between active brain and sleep.
Sleep
You fall asleep quickly with audio (podcasts, audiobooks) but wake multiple times in the night. True crime and learning content keeps you partially engaged and disrupts deep sleep. Low-stimulation audio works: reread audiobooks, sleep-focused podcasts, nature ambience, brown noise with a sleep timer. Phone goes to kitchen charger at 21:40, not the bedroom.
Sleep quality directly impacts next-day ADHD symptoms. Everything downstream (focus, task initiation, mood, motivation) gets worse on poor sleep. Consistent wake time (6:00 HYROX days, 6:30 rest days) is the single most important anchor for your circadian rhythm.
Physical Energy
HYROX 3x/week (Mon/Wed/Fri at 7am) provides a solid fitness base. The gap is movement between training sessions. You can sit for 4-6 hours without standing, which destroys afternoon energy. Hourly movement breaks and a mandatory midday walk are structural solutions. You want to lose weight and get more toned. You experience an afternoon energy slump that movement directly addresses.
Energy-Based Task Assignment
Rigid time blocks fail because your energy does not respect the clock. Some mornings you are sharp. Some mornings you are foggy. Energy-based assignment (high, medium, low tasks matched to current energy state) accommodates this variation. You still have blocks in your schedule, but the content flexes based on how you feel.
3. PRODUCTIVITY AND SYSTEMS
System Adoption Pattern
You have tried and abandoned multiple productivity systems. The pattern: find a new system, get excited, set it up elaborately, use it for 3-7 days, stop. The system fails not because it is bad, but because it requires too much daily maintenance, too many decisions, or too much willpower to sustain.
What Works
Extreme simplicity. One piece of paper. Three tasks. Fill it in the night before. Read it in the morning. Start.
Any system requiring more than 5 minutes of daily maintenance will be abandoned.
Decisions must be pre-made. If you wake up and have to decide what to work on, you have already lost 30-45 minutes to drifting.
Constraints. "You have 2.5 hours" produces better work than "work on this today." Fewer choices mean less friction.
Batch processing. Write 5 LinkedIn posts on Sunday, not one per day. Batch cook three times a week. Batch lead scraping in one session.
External accountability. Coworking, scheduled calls, social commitments, someone expecting you to show up.
Novelty and variety to sustain engagement. Rotating cooking projects, activity jar draws, different outbound angles.
The "suggest" button (when you do not know what to work on) and the "stuck" button (when you know but cannot start). These are ADHD-specific tools.
What Does Not Work
Journaling.
Rigid time blocks without energy flexibility.
Systems requiring daily upkeep beyond 5 minutes.
Working in silence.
Long periods of isolation.
Relying on willpower to make decisions in the moment.
Open evenings with no default activity.
Open-ended timelines with self-imposed deadlines.
Large multi-step plans without forced decision points.
Procrastination Patterns
You procrastinate through productive-feeling activities: planning, researching, organising, building systems, consuming content. These feel like work but delay the uncomfortable tasks that actually move the needle. Your instinct when uncertain is to consume more information. The corrective: you already know enough. Build, send, get feedback, adjust.
You procrastinate until pressure creates urgency. Client deadlines get met. Self-imposed deadlines do not. External accountability is disproportionately valuable for you.
4. COMMUNICATION AND PERSONALITY
Natural Style
Warm, direct, and conversational. You do not speak in corporate language. Your natural tone is casual, personal, and honest. This is a strength in sales and relationship-building. People trust you quickly because you sound like a real person.
Social Initiation
You connect easily once you are in the room. The barrier is getting yourself there. Once at an event, dinner, or call, you are engaging and likeable. The isolation pattern is not because you are bad with people. It is because initiating social contact requires the same executive function that makes task initiation hard. It is the same ADHD friction applied to your social life.
Hosting works better than going out. When you host (braai, dinner, game night, cookbook club), you control the environment, timing, and effort level. People come to you. This lowers the initiation barrier significantly.
Feedback Preferences
You prefer honesty over encouragement. Vague positivity does not land and can feel patronising. You would rather hear "this specific thing is not working, here is why, here is what to do instead." Direct feedback, even when uncomfortable, builds trust. Sugarcoating erodes it.
You are highly sensitive to shame-based criticism. Constructive feedback lands well. Character attacks or scapegoating trigger a protective withdrawal pattern. You need psychologically safe environments where feedback is about the work, not about your identity.
Self-Awareness
You are exceptionally self-aware. You can articulate your problems, patterns, and blind spots with precision. The gap is not awareness. It is action. You do not need more insight. You need systems that make the right action easier than the wrong one.

External deadlines and commitments. "I told a client I would deliver by Friday" gets done. "I should finish this by Friday" probably will not.
Competition, especially with yourself. HYROX appeals partly because it is measurable. Scorecard metrics tap the same drive.

Vague goals with no clear finish line.
Isolation for extended periods.
Work that feels mundane, draining, or unfulfilling.
Environments where criticism is personal rather than constructive.
Open-ended projects with no definition of done.
Being micromanaged or having authority undermined.

9. ENTREPRENEURIAL STYLE
Phase-Based Founder
You move best through discovery, proof, then amplification. You are not anti-execution. You are anti-being-the-bottleneck. You are not anti-leadership. You are anti-ambiguous leadership.
Phase 1 (Discovery): Identifying real problems, seeing patterns, evaluating what works, designing a constrained test. You excel here and tend to linger here because it is comfortable.
Phase 2 (Build): Building the core system, curating what works, turning proof into a clear offer. You can execute early but must exit execution on purpose.
Phase 3 (Amplify): Teaching, creating trust through clarity, building a brand around standards and outcomes, scaling through systems and partners. You excel here once conviction is earned.
Zones of Operation
Zone of genius (stay here): Seeing what matters and discarding noise. Identifying what truly works vs what sounds good. Distilling chaos into systems people can rely on. Designing the rule, standard, or guardrail. Translating proof into conviction others can borrow. Being an ethical filter in noisy markets.
Zone of excellence (high value, productise or bottleneck risk): Strategy and diagnosis. High-touch client problem solving. Custom solution design. Workflow and automation building. Graphic design.
Zone of competence (temporary, then delegate): Tactical implementation once the system is designed. Short-term execution with tight scope. One-off problem solving.
Zone of incompetence (avoid structurally): Admin-heavy SOP writing and upkeep. Naming conventions and compliance policing. Firefighting and escalation handling. Managing junior teams without strong systems.
Risk-Side Patterns to Watch
Perfectionism that delays shipping. Your first campaign will not be your best. Ship it.
Choosing content-consumption over execution when feeling self-doubt.
Starting strong then losing momentum when novelty wears off.
Chasing dopamine cycles: jumping between projects in an unproductive spiral.
Getting stuck in shame spirals over inconsistency.
Periods of huge energy followed by burnout.
Overwhelm when asked to commit to a large build. Break it into small forced decision points.
Seeking the perfect direction or external approval before moving.
Scope creep driven by people-pleasing on client calls.
