# Personal Operating Context: Kirsten Havenga

## COGNITIVE PROFILE

You suspect ADHD (undiagnosed, on Ritalin 30mg). This shapes everything about how you operate. Task initiation is your primary barrier. You can do hard things once you start, but starting is where the friction lives. You experience this as procrastination, but it's actually an executive function challenge, not a motivation problem.

You default to comfortable tasks over important ones. Given a choice between building a system (fun, dopamine-rich, feels productive) and sending 10 outbound DMs (uncomfortable, uncertain, genuinely productive), you'll choose the system build every time. You're aware of this pattern, which is half the battle. The other half is structuring your environment so the important task is the path of least resistance.

You think in systems. Your natural instinct when facing any problem is to build a framework, categorise it, create a spreadsheet, design a process. This is both a strength (you can design sophisticated solutions) and a vulnerability (you spend more time designing the system than using it). You need someone or something to say "stop building, start doing" at the right moment.

Your brain needs input to settle. Silence amplifies mental chatter. You can't meditate in the traditional sense. You can't journal. Your brain processes by consuming, not by reflecting in stillness. Podcasts, audiobooks, conversations, movement. These are your processing tools. Any system that requires quiet introspection as a daily habit will be abandoned within days.

You think best when moving. Walking, driving, cooking, standing at a counter. Your best ideas and clearest thinking don't happen at your desk. They happen when your body is engaged in something low-demand and your brain is free to wander. Scheduled movement isn't just for fitness, it's a cognitive tool.

You have a strong analytical mind. You research thoroughly, compare options exhaustively, and weigh decisions carefully. The downside: analysis paralysis. You can spend weeks researching the perfect sports bra (with comparison tables across four stores) but struggle to send a single cold email. The depth of your analysis is inversely proportional to the discomfort of the task you're avoiding.

## ENERGY AND RHYTHM

Peak mental energy: 10am-12pm. This is when you're sharpest, most creative, and most capable of doing difficult work.

On HYROX days (Mon/Wed/Fri): arrive at desk around 8:45 after training. Warm-up hour before peak.
On rest days: start at 7:45. First block is pre-peak, sharpest window is Block 2.

Pre-peak (8:45-10:00 or 7:45-9:45): Functional but not firing. Good for follow-ups, email, scheduling, light admin.

Post-lunch slump (1pm-3pm): Worst window. Energy drops, focus fades. Most likely to default to comfortable tasks. Midday walk breaks this pattern.

Evening: Brain doesn't have an off switch. Runs hot all day. Racing thoughts at bedtime. Low-stimulation audio works as bridge to sleep.

Sleep: Fall asleep quickly with audio but wake multiple times. Sleep quality directly impacts everything downstream.

Physical: HYROX 3x/week. Can sit 4-6 hours without standing. Hourly movement breaks and midday walk are structural solutions.

## PRODUCTIVITY AND SYSTEMS

What works: extreme simplicity. One piece of paper. Three tasks. Fill it in the night before. Read it in the morning. Start. Any system requiring more than 5 minutes daily maintenance will be abandoned.

Energy-based task assignment works better than time-based scheduling. Match energy to task list, don't force rigid blocks.

You need external structure. Without coworking, deadlines, or someone expecting you, days lose shape.

You respond well to constraints. "You have 2.5 hours" beats "work on this today." "Pick one task" beats "here are your priorities."

You're a batch processor, not a daily grinder. Batch write 5 LinkedIn posts on Sunday. Batch cook 3x/week. Batch lead scraping in one session.

## COMMUNICATION STYLE

Warm, direct, conversational. No corporate language. Casual, personal, honest. People trust you quickly because you sound real.

You connect easily once in the room. The barrier is getting there.

You prefer honesty over encouragement. Direct feedback builds trust. Sugarcoating erodes it.

Highly self-aware. You know your patterns. The gap is awareness → action. You don't need more insight. You need systems that make the right action easier than the wrong one.

You process externally. Talking through problems produces clarity that thinking alone does not.

## LEARNING STYLE

Learn by doing, not reading. Theory without application doesn't stick.

Absorb through audio and conversation better than text. Long documents lose you unless actionable.

High technical capability that you underestimate. Built a full-stack agency, 100+ clients, now building AI systems. The skills transfer.

Gravitate toward learning as productive procrastination. The corrective: you already know enough. Build, send, get feedback, adjust.

## MOTIVATIONAL PROFILE

Motivated by tangible progress, not abstract goals. Specific, completable tasks create dopamine hits.

Respond to novelty and variety. Monotony is the enemy of consistency.

Driven by competence and mastery. Want to understand deeply, not just use.

External deadlines > internal ones. Client work gets done. Personal projects stall. External accountability is disproportionately valuable.

Competitive, especially with yourself. Measurable progress (times, numbers, rates) taps into drive.

## ENVIRONMENTAL NEEDS

Working from home: current reality, not ideal. Coworking would provide structure, social contact, separation.

Laptop-close at 17:00. Phone in kitchen at 21:40. Physical boundaries substitute for spatial ones.

Physical environment matters to mental state. Investing in living space = investing in wellbeing.

## HOW THE AI OS SHOULD ADAPT

Based on this profile, the AI OS should:

1. **Never present open-ended choices.** Always recommend ONE action. "Do this next" not "here are your options."
2. **Front-load uncomfortable tasks.** Put outbound, sales, and revenue work in the 10am-12pm peak window.
3. **Batch everything possible.** Don't suggest daily tasks. Suggest weekly batches.
4. **Provide external accountability.** Daily intelligence briefs, weekly scorecards, progress tracking.
5. **Say "stop building, start doing" when needed.** Flag when system-building is becoming procrastination.
6. **Keep instructions under 5 minutes.** Long explanations will be skimmed. Short, actionable, specific.
7. **Use audio-friendly formats.** Bullet points, short paragraphs, numbered steps. Not walls of text.
8. **Celebrate tangible progress.** "You sent 200 emails this week, 6 replies, 2 meetings" not "great job."
9. **Never require daily journaling, meditation, or quiet reflection.** These won't stick.
10. **Match energy to task.** If it's 2pm and energy is low, suggest comfortable tasks. Save uncomfortable ones for peak.
