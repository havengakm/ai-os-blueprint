## By Email Purpose

### First-touch cold email (you have never contacted them)

Best patterns: City + vertical, Company name + asset, Industry shorthand
Avoid: Anything with "follow up" or "re:" â€” you have no prior thread

Examples:
- `phoenix property management`
- `{{company}}'s site`
- `5 videos for {{firstName}}`

### Follow-up email (sent 4-7 days after first email, no reply)

Best patterns: Quick question, Their last action, Single word
Avoid: Re-stating the original offer in the subject

Examples:
- `quick question`
- `last try`
- `one more thought`

### Reply-bait email (sent 14+ days after, last attempt)

Best patterns: Direct, slightly emotional, single word
Goal: Get a "no, not interested" reply (which is still data) or break through the silence

Examples:
- `closing the loop`
- `last attempt`
- `should i stop`

### Post-event email (after they attended a conference, webinar, etc.)

Best patterns: Event name reference, mutual context

Examples:
- `narpm broker/owner`
- `your panel comment`
- `the appfolio session`

### Signal-triggered email (after specific signal detected)

Best patterns: Their last action, mutual context

Examples:
- `your phoenix expansion`
- `the growth lead role`
- `your bigger pockets question`

## By Audience Seniority

### C-suite / Founder / Owner

Best patterns: Question for {{firstName}}, Industry shorthand, Their company name
Why: Senior buyers respond to peer-feeling subjects. Want minimal salesy energy.

Examples:
- `question for sarah`
- `{{company}}'s pipeline`
- `icp question`

### VP / Director level

Best patterns: Number + deliverable, Their company name + asset
Why: Metric-driven. Specific numbers and assets create immediate relevance.

Examples:
- `200 accounts for {{firstName}}`
- `{{company}}'s outbound`
- `{{company}}'s site`

### Manager / Operator level

Best patterns: Their last action, City + vertical
Why: Tactical buyers. Want specific tactical relevance.

Examples:
- `your phoenix expansion`
- `phoenix property management`
- `the growth lead role`

## By Industry Sophistication

### High-sophistication audiences (B2B SaaS, AI, marketing, finance)

Use: Industry shorthand, ultra-short subjects, single words
Avoid: Anything that could appear in a marketing email

Examples:
- `icp question`
- `aov question`
- `quick question`
- `last try`

These audiences receive 30+ cold emails per week and have learned to spot patterns instantly. The shorter and more mundane, the better.

### Medium-sophistication audiences (PM, real estate, recruiting, professional services)

Use: City + vertical, Company name + asset, Number + deliverable
Avoid: Industry insider shorthand they may not recognize

Examples:
- `phoenix property management`
- `{{company}}'s site`
- `5 videos for {{firstName}}`

### Lower-sophistication audiences (small local businesses, brick-and-mortar)

Use: Their company name, direct simple subjects
Avoid: Industry shorthand entirely; they will not get it

Examples:
- `{{company}}'s reviews`
- `your business`
- `quick question`

## Single-Word Subject Lines (Use Sparingly)

Single-word subjects can perform exceptionally well or terribly. Test before scaling.

Patterns that sometimes work:
- `?`
- `hi`
- `question`
- `interested`
- `thoughts`

Patterns that almost always work as follow-ups:
- `bump`
- `?`
- `bumping`
- `closing`

Single-word subjects work because they look like internal team emails. Risk: spam filters can flag them as low-effort. Test deliverability first on small samples (50-100) before scaling.

## Patterns Across All Categories

Looking at the subjects in every category, these meta-patterns hold:

1. **Lowercase wins.** Title case looks like marketing.
2. **Specificity beats cleverness.** "phoenix property management" beats "growing fast in PM."
3. **Length under 5 words.** 2-3 word subjects outperform 4-6 word subjects in most tests.
4. **Match the recipient's expectation.** Senior buyers want short and direct. Junior buyers tolerate slightly more context.
5. **Reference what is real.** Faking specifics destroys credibility on opens (when they read the body and find no match).

## Subject Lines for Different Funnel Stages

### Top of funnel (cold, no signals)

Default to: City + vertical, Company name + asset

Why: You have minimal information; the subject must work on minimal data.

### Middle of funnel (signal detected)

Default to: Their last action, Mutual context

Why: Real signal lets you match the subject to their current attention.

### Re-engagement (previous outreach, no reply)

Default to: Quick question, Single word follow-ups

Why: Familiarity (they have seen your name before) lets you go ultra-short.

## Anti-Patterns: Subjects That Look Good But Fail

These look like winning subjects but consistently underperform in testing.

1. **`Quick win for {{company}}`** â€” "Quick win" is overused. Pattern-matched as automation.

2. **`{{firstName}}, you're missing out`** â€” Manufactured FOMO. Aggressive. Triggers skepticism.

3. **`Idea for {{company}}`** â€” Vague. Recipient assumes it is unsolicited advice.

4. **`{{firstName}} - Important`** â€” "Important" is a spam trigger word. Avoid.

5. **`Helping {{company}} grow`** â€” Sounds like every marketing email ever sent.

6. **`Following up on our previous conversation`** â€” Lying about prior contact. Trust-killer.

7. **`Special offer for {{firstName}}`** â€” Spam trigger words. Avoid completely.

8. **`Last chance to...`** â€” Manufactured urgency. Senior buyers ignore these.

9. **`A few thoughts on your...`** â€” Sounds presumptuous. Vague.

10. **`Just wanted to reach out`** â€” ClichĂ©. Pattern-matched as automated.
