# Cold Email Body Writer

Write complete cold email bodies that convert because the offer does the selling, not the words. Uses Problem → Solution → Proof → CTA structure with a tangible free deliverable as the call to action.

## When to use this skill

Trigger this skill when the user wants to:

- Write a complete cold email body (everything after the personalized opener)
- Generate subject lines for cold email campaigns
- Build cold email templates for B2B outreach
- Create full email sequences for Instantly, Smartlead, Lemlist, or similar tools
- Design a low-friction CTA with a tangible free deliverable
- Improve reply rates on existing cold emails

This skill pairs naturally with `intent-opener-writer`, which produces the {{personalization}} line. This skill produces everything else.

## The core principle

A cold email body in 2026 is not a sales pitch. It is a clean, scannable structure that makes one specific offer that is so valuable saying no would feel like a mistake. The body itself does almost no persuasion. The offer carries the entire email.

If the offer is weak, no amount of clever copy saves the email. If the offer is strong, simple direct copy outperforms clever copy every time.

## Required structure

Every cold email body must follow this exact structure. Do not deviate.

```
Subject: [3-5 words, lowercase, specific to recipient]

Hi {{firstName}},

{{personalization}}

[PROBLEM — 1-2 sentences naming a specific pain or pattern in their world]

[SOLUTION — 2-4 sentences. What you do, what is included, what they have to do]

[PROOF — 1 sentence with a specific number]

[CTA — 1-2 sentences offering a tangible free deliverable]

[QUESTION — 2-4 words]

[SIGN-OFF — name, optionally role @ company]
```

Total body length: 70-110 words after the personalization line. Aggressively short. Mobile-readable in 6-8 seconds.

## Subject line rules

Subject lines determine open rate. Open rate determines everything else.

### Winning subject line patterns

Use one of these five patterns. They all win because they sound human, not promotional.

1. **Quick question** — 2 words, casual, peer-to-peer. Works for high-trust scenarios.
2. **[Brand name]'s website** — references their specific company. Curiosity-trigger.
3. **Question for [first name]** — direct, personalized. Implies a real question.
4. **[N] [deliverable] for [first name]** — names the value. Example: "5 videos for Sarah."
5. **[City] [vertical]** — geo + niche. Example: "Phoenix med spa." Implies relevance.

### Banned subject line patterns

These signal automated outreach immediately and hurt open rates and deliverability:

- Capitalized titles ("Quick Question About Your Business")
- Exclamation points ("Boost Your Conversions!")
- Promotional words: "boost," "improve," "grow," "scale," "double," "transform," "10x"
- "Re:" or "Fwd:" fakes (deliverability risk and trust killer)
- Subject lines longer than 6 words
- Anything that sounds like a marketing email

### Subject line construction

Match the subject to the offer in the CTA. If the CTA offers "5 video scripts," the subject is "5 videos for [name]." If the CTA offers a "landing page mockup," the subject might be "[brand]'s site." This makes the email feel coherent end-to-end.

## Problem section rules

One to two sentences. Names a specific pattern, not a generic complaint. The recipient should read the problem and think "that is exactly my situation," not "everyone has that problem."

### Strong problem patterns

- **Industry observation:** "Most [audience] in [city] are [specific behavior that fails them]." Example: "Most med spas running ads in Miami are spending money on campaigns that attract price shoppers, not the high-value patients who book Botox, filler, and memberships."

- **Direct work observation:** Reference something specific you saw on their website, ads, or content. Example: "Looked at your last three property listings on Zillow — they are using stock photos where short video would convert way better."

### Weak problem patterns to avoid

- Generic pain: "You probably struggle with marketing." (Everyone says this. It triggers skepticism.)
- Surveillance creep: "I noticed your conversion rate seems low." (Even if you can detect it, do not say it.)
- Manufactured urgency: "Your competitors are eating your lunch." (Sounds aggressive.)
- Backhanded compliments: "Great business, but you could be doing so much more." (Insulting.)

## Solution section rules

Two to four sentences. Three components in order:

1. **What you do** in plain language. One sentence.
2. **What is included** as a specific list. Use commas, no bullets. Example: "We handle everything: scripts, filming, direction, and editing."
3. **What they have to do.** Make it minimal. Example: "You just show up." or "We need 30 minutes of your time."

### Strong solution patterns

- "I help [audience] [specific outcome]. We handle [list]. You just [minimal effort]."
- "I run a [type] agency. One thing working well for similar [audience] is [specific tactic]."
- "I build [deliverable] for [audience] — [specific differentiator]."

The "we handle everything, you just show up" phrasing is gold because it removes effort objection in advance.

## Proof section rules

One sentence. Always contains a specific number.

### Strong proof patterns

- "We have helped 87+ agents go viral and build a consistent pipeline from content in under two hours a month."
- "I have helped three practices in California cut their cost-per-lead in half while doubling average patient value."
- "Worked with 100+ fitness studios across 10 countries before launching this."

### What to do if you do not have proof yet

If the user is new and has no client results yet, do not invent stats. Use one of these honest substitutes:

- **Personal experience proof:** "Personally analyzed 50+ [audience] [type of analysis] last month."
- **Adjacent experience proof:** "Built and exited a [type] agency that worked with 100+ [adjacent audience] across 10 countries."
- **Process proof:** "Built this same system for [N] [adjacent audience] before pivoting to [current audience]."
- **Skip proof:** Replace with a confidence statement: "Built around what is currently converting for [audience] in your market right now."

Never use vague claims like "we have helped many businesses" or "trusted by industry leaders." Specificity is everything.

## CTA section rules

This is the most important section. The CTA is the entire email.

### The CTA must offer a tangible free deliverable

A "free deliverable" is something specific the recipient receives whether they hire you or not. It is not a meeting. It is not a consultation. It is not an audit. It is a finished output they can use immediately.

### Strong CTA patterns

- "I'd love to [verb] [specific deliverable with number] for [their company name] — completely free."
- "I'd love to build you a [specific deliverable] for one of your [products/listings/locations] — practical enough your team could use it this week."
- "I'd love to [first step of your normal process] for [their company] — built around what is actually working for [audience] in your market."

### Examples of strong free deliverables by service type

- **Video/content services:** "5 video scripts mapped to your business," "3 hooks tested for your audience"
- **Ad services:** "3 proven ad creatives plus copy you could run on your own"
- **CRO/landing pages:** "A landing page mockup for one of your top products"
- **Lead gen/outbound:** "A target list of 50 prospects matched to your ICP"
- **SEO services:** "An SEO audit of your top 10 pages with prioritized fixes"
- **Strategy/consulting:** "A 90-day growth plan for your [specific business situation]"
- **AI/automation:** "A custom workflow blueprint mapping 3 of your manual processes"

### What makes a free deliverable strong

A strong free deliverable has all four properties:

1. **Tangible.** It is a thing they can hold, view, or use. Not a meeting.
2. **Specific.** It has a number ("5 scripts," "3 ads," "10 pages"), not vague scope.
3. **Valuable on its own.** They could use it immediately, even without hiring you.
4. **Proves your capability.** Doing it well demonstrates you can deliver the paid version.

### Weak CTAs to avoid

- "Open to a quick chat?" — meeting requests fail. Recipients have no reason to give time.
- "Free 15-minute consultation" — discovery calls in disguise. Pattern-matched.
- "Free audit" — audits are easy to fake and recipients know it. They want output, not analysis.
- "Get on a call to learn more" — the value is undefined. They will not bite.

If the offer is something they could get from any vendor, replace it. The free deliverable should be specific enough that copying it across vendors would be obvious.

## Question section rules

After the CTA, ask one short closing question. Two to four words. Lowercase or sentence case.

Strong:
- "Interested?"
- "Would you be open to that?"
- "Want me to send it over?"
- "Worth a quick look?"

Weak:
- "Looking forward to hearing from you!" (not a question, sounds eager)
- "Please let me know if this is something you would like to explore further" (too long)
- Anything with multiple sentences

## Sign-off rules

Keep it minimal. Either:

- Just the first name: "Austin"
- First name with role and company: "Jake / Creative Strategist @ ListingBoost"

Do not include:

- "Best regards" or "Kind regards"
- Phone numbers in the body (creates clutter)
- Long signature blocks with logos and links (deliverability risk)

## Word count discipline

Total email length budget (after subject line and personalization opener):

- Problem: 15-25 words
- Solution: 25-40 words
- Proof: 12-20 words
- CTA: 18-30 words
- Question: 2-4 words
- Sign-off: 2-8 words

**Total: 70-110 words.** Hard ceiling at 130. If the email exceeds 130 words, cut, do not edit.

## Banned words and patterns

These pattern-match as automated outreach. Avoid all of them:

- "Hope this finds you well"
- "I came across your profile"
- "I wanted to reach out"
- "Just following up"
- "Touch base"
- "Circle back"
- "Synergy," "leverage," "ecosystem," "navigate," "delve," "landscape"
- "Game-changer," "10x," "unlock," "supercharge," "transform"
- "Best regards," "Warm regards," "Sincerely"
- Em dashes (use commas or periods)
- Multiple exclamation points
- Emojis (rare exceptions OK; default to none)

## Output format

When generating an email, produce it in this format:

```
Subject: [subject line]

Hi {{firstName}},

{{personalization}}

[problem section]

[solution section]

[proof section]

[CTA section]

[question]

[sign-off]
```

Keep `{{firstName}}` and `{{personalization}}` as literal placeholder strings. The user's outreach tool will replace them.

After the email, add a one-line summary:

```
Word count (excluding personalization and subject): [N]
Free deliverable offered: [specific deliverable]
```

## Worked examples

**Input:** User offers AI-powered owner acquisition system to PM companies. Target: 400-door PM company in Phoenix.

**Output:**

```

{{personalization}}

Most PMs your size in Phoenix are getting Sunday and Monday inquiries that go cold by Wednesday because nobody is responding fast enough. Owners sign with whoever calls back first.

I help PM companies install AI agents that respond to every inquiry in 60 seconds and run outbound to absentee landlords automatically. We handle the build, the integrations, and the ongoing optimization. You review one weekly dashboard.

If you are looking to grow Phoenix doors faster, I would love to build you a free target list of 200 absentee landlords matched to your portfolio profile. Useful whether you work with us or not.

Worth a look?

Sebastiaan

Word count (excluding personalization and subject): 108
Free deliverable offered: Target list of 200 absentee landlords matched to their ICP
```

## Quality checklist

Before finalizing any email, verify:

- [ ] Subject line is 3-5 words, lowercase, no exclamation
- [ ] Total body word count is 70-110 (hard ceiling 130)
- [ ] Problem references something specific to their world, not generic pain
- [ ] Solution includes "what you do," "what is included," and "what they do" (minimal)
- [ ] Proof contains a specific number (or honest substitute)
- [ ] CTA offers a tangible free deliverable, not a meeting
- [ ] Free deliverable has a number ("5 scripts," not "some scripts")
- [ ] Question is 2-4 words
- [ ] Zero banned words
- [ ] No em dashes
- [ ] Reads on mobile in 6-8 seconds

## A/B testing recommendation

When the user is launching a campaign, suggest testing variants on these axes (in order of impact on reply rate):

1. **The free deliverable.** This is the highest-leverage variable. Test 2-3 different free deliverables in parallel.
2. **Subject line.** Test "[city] [vertical]" against "[N] [deliverable] for [name]."
3. **Problem framing.** Test direct observation vs. industry pattern.
4. **Proof angle.** Test specific result number vs. specific scale number.

Do not test multiple variables at once. Change one thing per A/B test.

## Common failure patterns to avoid

When reviewing AI-generated emails, watch for these failures:

1. **Vague free deliverable.** "Free strategy session" instead of "free 5-video script set." Fix by demanding specificity and a number.

2. **Meeting CTA disguised as a deliverable.** "Free 15-minute audit call" is still a meeting. Fix by making the CTA a thing they receive, not a thing they attend.

3. **No proof number.** "We have worked with many ecom brands." Fix by replacing with specific count or removing the proof line entirely.

4. **Generic problem.** "You are probably struggling with conversion." Fix by tying it to a specific industry pattern or observation.

5. **Solution drift.** Solution section runs 60+ words. Fix by cutting to "what you do, what is included, what they do."

6. **Long question.** "Would you be open to perhaps exploring this further at your convenience?" Fix by cutting to "Interested?" or "Worth a look?"

7. **Promotional subject line.** "Boost Your Phoenix Property Management Business!" Fix by using the lowercase 3-5 word patterns.
